Young Malaysia Under Pressure

A visual story of how job pressure, living costs, and household spending combine differently across Malaysian states.

For young Malaysians, financial pressure is not caused by one factor alone. A state may have a low unemployment rate but higher living costs, while another may have weaker labour conditions but lower spending pressure. This visualisation follows that story step by step: first by looking at youth unemployment, then comparing state labour conditions, then exploring cost-of-living changes, and finally combining these signals into a custom pressure index.

1. Are young Malaysians still facing job pressure?

The story begins with youth unemployment because employment is often the first pressure point for young people entering the labour market. This line chart uses monthly youth unemployment data and displays it across years to make the long-term pattern easier to follow. The sharp movement around 2020 helps show how youth labour conditions changed during the pandemic period and how slowly the rate moved back down afterwards.

2. Which youth age group faces higher unemployment?

Youth unemployment is not experienced equally by all young people. This comparison separates the narrower 15–24 age group from the broader 15–30 group. The aim is to show whether younger youth face stronger job pressure than older youth who may have more work experience, qualifications or labour-market stability.

3. Where is labour pressure highest?

After looking at national youth trends, the visualisation moves to state-level labour pressure. The choropleth map gives a geographic view of unemployment across Malaysia, while the supporting charts below explain the ranking, participation pattern and labour force composition behind the map. Together, these visuals show that labour pressure is not evenly distributed across the country.

4. Where is daily life becoming more expensive?

Employment pressure is only one side of the problem. Even when people are employed, rising prices can reduce how far household income can go. This section uses CPI data to compare cost pressure across states and spending categories. Instead of only using raw CPI values, the heatmap calculates the percentage change between Jan 2024 and Apr 2026. Darker orange means a larger CPI increase over that period.

5. Does income keep up with spending?

Cost pressure becomes more meaningful when it is compared with household income and spending. This section uses the Household Income and Expenditure Survey state data to compare income and expenditure across Malaysia. A derived spending-pressure ratio is calculated by dividing mean expenditure by mean income. This makes the comparison fairer because it shows how much of average income is being taken up by spending, rather than only comparing raw income or raw expenditure values.

6. Which states face the strongest combined pressure?

The final section brings the earlier ideas together. Labour pressure, CPI cost pressure and spending pressure are converted into normalised scores and combined into one custom pressure index. This is not an official government ranking. It is a visual storytelling score created for this project to compare how different types of pressure overlap across Malaysian states.

How the combined pressure score is calculated

The combined pressure score uses three indicators: unemployment pressure, CPI cost pressure, and spending pressure. Since these values use different units, each one is first converted into a normalised score from 0 to 100. A higher score means stronger pressure compared with other Malaysian states in this visualisation.

1. Unemployment score

Uses the latest state unemployment rate from Q3 2025.

(state unemployment rate - lowest unemployment rate) / (highest unemployment rate - lowest unemployment rate) × 100

2. CPI score

Uses the derived CPI percentage change from Jan 2024 to Apr 2026.

(state CPI change - lowest CPI change) / (highest CPI change - lowest CPI change) × 100

3. Spending score

Uses the expenditure-to-income ratio from the Household Income and Expenditure Survey 2022.

(state spending ratio - lowest spending ratio) / (highest spending ratio - lowest spending ratio) × 100

Final combined pressure score

(unemployment score + CPI score + spending score) / 3

Example: if a state has an unemployment score of 25.6, a CPI score of 100.0, and a spending score of 57.3, then the combined score is (25.6 + 100.0 + 57.3) / 3 = 61.0.

Data sources and derived files

The visualisation uses official open datasets from data.gov.my and a Malaysia GeoJSON boundary file. Some smaller derived CSV files were created from the original datasets to make the Vega-Lite joins and custom score calculations clearer and faster.

Original data sources

Household Income and Expenditure Survey state data: https://data.gov.my/data-catalogue/hies_state

Consumer Price Index by state: https://data.gov.my/data-catalogue/cpi_state

Youth labour force data: https://data.gov.my/data-catalogue/lfs_month_youth

Malaysia state GeoJSON boundary file: https://github.com/mptwaktusolat/jakim.geojson/blob/master/malaysia.state.geojson

Derived CSV files used in the project

lfs_state_latest.csv: A filtered version of the quarterly labour force data containing only the latest available state-level records. It is used in the Malaysia unemployment map and the state labour-pressure charts so that each state has one current value.

pressure_index.csv: A custom derived dataset created for the final section. It combines state unemployment pressure, CPI percentage change and spending-pressure ratio into normalised component scores and a final combined pressure score.
